EYEBROW: The starting point · what existed

H2: From agent management to [agent oversight]
  (ה-italic: "agent oversight")

LEDE:
This wasn't a technology problem, it was a trust gap. The product could manage
agents; what was missing was the oversight layer that made autonomy feel safe
to turn on.

עמודה שמאל: Existing layer:
- Create / Run / View activity / Edit workflows

עמודה ימין: What was missing:
- See pending decisions across all agents at once
- Understand blast radius before an action runs
- Review and approve at scale, not agent by agent
- Know ownership, scope and risk for every agent

[N] Speaker note (לך בלבד):
This is the core reframing of the case study. The product already had agent
management: create, run, view activity and edit workflows. What was missing
was the oversight layer: the layer that helps operators understand what agents
are doing, what needs attention, what is risky, and whether they can safely
step in.

[N] Speaker notes (עברית):
השקף הזה מציג את שיקולי הדיזיין מאחורי הפתרון, לא רק מה שיניתי, אלא מה בחרתי להעדיף ועל מה ויתרתי בדרך.
Visibility vs. density: בחרתי להביא את ההחלטות שמחכות לאישור לתוך המסך הראשי. המחיר: צפיפות. אבל אישור קריטי לא אמור להישאר חבוי.
Context vs. speed: בחרתי להציג רק את הקונטקסט הנדרש. המחיר: לא כל הפרטים מיד. אבל זה מאפשר סריקה מהירה של הרבה אג׳נטים.
Autonomy vs. control: שמרתי על נקודות התערבות ברורות: review, snooze, hand off. המחיר: עוד שלב. אבל זה עוזר למשתמשים להרגיש בטוחים יותר להפעיל אג׳נטים.
משפט מרכזי: בכל החלטה היה trade-off: צפיפות לטובת נראות, פחות פרטים לטובת סריקה, נקודות התערבות לטובת תחושת שליטה.

H2: Five principles for designing [trust] in agents
  (ה-italic: "trust")

5 PRINCIPLES (כותרת | תיאור | source):
1. Intent Preview          | Show what will happen before it happens.           | NN/g · #1 trust driver
2. Action Audit + Undo     | Every action logged, readable, reversible.          | Cuts perceived risk
3. Variable Autonomy       | Not on/off, a spectrum: auto · approve-per-step · sandbox. | MS · +mental model
4. Confidence Signals      | Show how sure it is, and why.                      | NN/g · trust ↑
5. Single Pane of Glass    | See and act on every agent from one view.           | Microsoft

[N] Backed by: 62% of orgs piloting agents (McKinsey '25) · 40% of agent
  projects scrapped by '27 for trust & UX, not tech (Gartner) · 79% prefer
  a human when things get complex (SurveyMonkey '25).

H2: Pains → ideas → solutions

CELL 1 [הציטוט]:
HEADER: Pain 01
QUOTE: "To approve anything, I have to go agent by agent."

CELL 2 [רעיונות]:
HEADER: Ideas I weighed
• A badge on the agent's row
• A notification / toast
• [✓] Surface decisions at list level
• [✓] "Decisions Waiting" as entry point

CELL 3 [פתרון]:
HEADER: How I solved it
• [✓] "Decisions Waiting" metric doubles as the review entry point: oldest flagged first
• [✓] Snooze or hand off to the right person (who gets an email with full context)
• [✓] Agents shared across the team: oversight never stuck with one person

ROW 2 - ADDITIONAL PAINS:
Pain 02: No sense of blast radius
  How many resources? What's the cost impact? Is it reversible? None of this was visible before acting.

Pain 03: Approval didn't scale
  Five pending decisions meant five separate screens, one agent at a time.

Pain 04: Status was ambiguous
  Running? Waiting for a trigger? Failed silently? There was no clear status signal at the list level.

RESEARCH BACKING (footnote):
Gartner (2025): "Agents acting without user visibility create legal and operational exposure: the UI is the accountability layer."
NN/g (2026): "Trust is a design problem. Focus: legibility: visibility, intervenability, understanding the why."

EYEBROW: What I'd measure

H2: Three signals [I'd track]
  (ה-italic: "I'd track")

3 CARDS:

Agent activation rate
How many users who have agents actually turn them on.
This is the main signal: it measures whether the trust layer did its job.

Time-to-decision
How long it takes to approve or reject a pending decision.
Fast means the context was clear. Slow means something is still confusing.

Snooze rate
How often operators delay instead of deciding.
High snooze doesn't mean they're busy, it means they need more context before they act.
